What Is Fine-tuning?

The pre-train → fine-tune paradigm

Let's start with the fundamental concept.

The Two-Stage Paradigm

Stage 1: Pre-training
Learn language from billions of tokens (weeks on many GPUs)
↓
Stage 2: Fine-tuning
Adapt to your task with a small dataset (hours on one GPU)

Key insight: Fine-tuning works because pre-trained models already understand language. We just need to steer that knowledge toward our task.

This is the foundational idea. Pre-training is the expensive part that happens once (done by OpenAI, Meta, Google, etc.). Fine-tuning is the cheap part that you do for your specific application. Think of it like this: pre-training builds the engine, fine-tuning adjusts the steering. The analogy with transfer learning in computer vision is exact: ImageNet pre-training, then fine-tune for your specific image classification task.

An Analogy

Pre-training is like getting a university degree — broad knowledge of the world.

Fine-tuning is like job training — specialized skills for a specific role.

Pre-training teaches

  • Grammar and syntax
  • World knowledge
  • Reasoning patterns

Fine-tuning teaches

  • Your specific format
  • Your domain vocabulary
  • Your task objectives
This analogy resonates well with engineers. You wouldn't teach a new hire to read and write -- you assume they already can. You teach them your codebase, your processes, your domain. Same idea with fine-tuning: the model already knows language, you teach it your task.

Full Fine-tuning

The classic approach — and its costs

Let's start with the simplest form of fine-tuning: update everything.

Training Loop: Key Concepts Visualized

① All Parameters
Every weight in every layer is updated — GPT-3: 175B weights.
② Forward Pass
Batch → network → prediction → compute Loss
③ Backpropagation
Chain rule → ∂L/∂W for every weight — "how much did this weight cause the error?"
④ Learning Rate η
W ← W − η × ∂L/∂W  |  Fine-tuning: η = 2e-5 (tiny — preserve pre-trained knowledge)
⑤ Epoch
1 epoch = 1 full pass through training data  |  Fine-tuning: 1–5 epochs (more → overfitting)
Walk through step by step. The network starts with pre-trained weights. Fragment 1: ALL weights (hidden layers highlighted) are trainable — not just the last layer. Fragment 2: Feed a data batch forward, get a prediction, compute the Loss. Fragment 3: Backpropagation uses the chain rule to compute the gradient ∂L/∂W for every single weight in the network — how much each weight contributed to the error. Fragment 4: The learning rate controls step size. In fine-tuning we use a tiny η (2e-5) compared to pre-training (1e-4) because we want to gently steer, not destroy. Fragment 5: One epoch = one complete pass through your fine-tuning dataset. With full fine-tuning, ALL weights are updated every batch, every epoch — that's why memory costs explode.

Full fine-tuning is conceptually simple: take the pre-trained model and run standard backprop on your task data. The small learning rate (2e-5 vs 1e-4) is critical — you want to steer, not destroy. But the cost is steep: 7B parameters × 4 bytes = 28GB just for weights; add gradients, Adam optimizer states, and activations and you're over 100GB. Worse, each task needs its own full copy. This drove the field toward PEFT methods.

This visual makes the problem obvious. Each bar is a full model copy. The models are 99.9% identical -- they differ only slightly in their learned weights. It's incredibly wasteful. We'll see that LoRA solves this by storing only the tiny differences (a few MB per task instead of 14GB).

Instruction Tuning

Teaching models to follow instructions

Before we get to parameter-efficient methods, let's talk about a specific TYPE of fine-tuning that changed everything: instruction tuning. This is how ChatGPT-style models learned to be helpful.

What Is Instruction Tuning?

Train on (instruction, output) pairs to make models follow commands

Instruction:
Summarize the following article in 3 bullet points:
[article text...]
Output:
• First key point...
• Second key point...
• Third key point...

Before instruction tuning: model completes text (autocomplete)
After instruction tuning: model follows instructions (assistant)

This is the key insight. A raw pre-trained GPT model is an autocomplete engine. If you ask it a question, it might just generate more questions. Instruction tuning teaches it to actually answer. The training data format is simple: an instruction describing the task, and the desired output. You fine-tune the model on thousands of these pairs.

Key Instruction Datasets

Dataset Source Size Key Feature
FLAN Google 1,800+ tasks Converted NLP benchmarks into instruction format
Alpaca Stanford 52K GPT-3.5 generated, self-instruct method
Dolly Databricks 15K Human-written, commercially usable
OpenAssistant LAION 160K Multi-turn conversations, community-driven

These datasets enabled the open-source ChatGPT alternatives (LLaMA-Chat, Vicuna, etc.)

FLAN was the pioneer -- Google took existing NLP datasets and reformatted them as instructions. Alpaca was remarkable because Stanford used GPT-3.5 to generate 52K instruction pairs for just $500, then fine-tuned LLaMA on them. This "self-instruct" approach showed you could create instruction-following models cheaply. Dolly from Databricks was the first high-quality, human-written, commercially licensable dataset. OpenAssistant is community-driven with multi-turn conversations.

Why Instruction Tuning Matters

Base Model (no instruction tuning)

Input: What is the capital of France?

Output: What is the capital of Germany? What is the capital of Spain? What is the capital of...

Just autocompletes text

Instruction-tuned Model

Input: What is the capital of France?

Output: The capital of France is Paris. It is the largest city in France and serves as the country's political and cultural center.

Actually answers the question

This is the most convincing example of why instruction tuning matters. The base model has all the knowledge -- it knows Paris is the capital of France. But it doesn't know that when you ask a question, it should ANSWER it. Instruction tuning teaches this format: given a question, produce an answer. This is the difference between GPT-3 (base) and ChatGPT (instruction-tuned + RLHF).

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning

PEFT: Train less, achieve more

Now for the most important section of this session. PEFT methods solve the cost problem we discussed earlier. Instead of updating all 7 billion parameters, we update only a tiny fraction.

The PEFT Idea

Freeze the pre-trained model, train only a small number of new parameters

7B
Pre-trained params
(frozen)
+
~8M
New trainable params
(~0.1% of total)
  • Less memory: only store gradients for new parameters
  • Less storage: save only the small adapter, not the full model
  • Less risk: pre-trained knowledge stays intact (no catastrophic forgetting)
The core idea of PEFT is beautifully simple. The pre-trained model already knows a lot. We don't need to change all of it. We just add or modify a tiny number of parameters. Going from 7B trainable parameters to 8M is a 1000x reduction. This means you can fine-tune on a single consumer GPU.

LoRA: The Key Insight

Observation: Weight changes during fine-tuning have low intrinsic rank

When we fine-tune a weight matrix W (size d × d), the change ΔW can be approximated by a low-rank decomposition:

ΔW ≈ A × B

where A is (d × r) and B is (r × d), with r « d

Example: d = 4096, r = 8
Full ΔW: 4096 × 4096 = 16.7M params
LoRA (A + B): 4096 × 8 + 8 × 4096 = 65K params — 256× fewer!

Hu et al. (2021). "LoRA: Low-Rank Adaptation of Large Language Models" — arXiv:2106.09685

This is the crucial insight from the LoRA paper. When you fine-tune a large model, you don't actually need to change all dimensions of the weight matrices. The updates tend to live in a low-dimensional subspace. So instead of learning the full change matrix delta-W, we decompose it into two small matrices A and B. With rank r=8, this is a 256x parameter reduction for a single 4096x4096 weight matrix. Across the whole model, the savings are enormous.

Only A and B are trained. At inference, merge: W' = W + A·B (zero extra latency!)

Let me walk through this diagram. On the left, the original frozen weight matrix W processes the input as usual. On the right, the LoRA path: input goes through a small down-projection B (from d dimensions to r dimensions), then an up-projection A (back to d dimensions). The outputs are added together. The beautiful part: at inference time, you can merge A*B back into W, creating W' = W + A*B. This means zero additional latency at inference -- it's as if you fine-tuned the full model.

LoRA by the Numbers

Metric Full Fine-tuning LoRA (r=8)

875× less storage per task. 5 tasks = 80 MB instead of 70 GB.

Let these numbers sink in. From 14GB per task to 16MB. From 112GB GPU memory to 16GB -- that's a single consumer GPU like an RTX 4090. And performance is nearly identical to full fine-tuning. This is why LoRA became the de facto standard for fine-tuning LLMs. The rank r is a hyperparameter: r=8 is a good default, r=16 or r=32 if you need more expressiveness.

Dettmers et al. (2023). "QLoRA: Efficient Finetuning of Quantized Language Models" — arXiv:2305.14314

QLoRA pushes efficiency even further. The idea is simple: quantize the base model to 4-bit precision (so 7B params fit in about 3.5GB), then add LoRA adapters on top in higher precision. The three key innovations from the paper: 4-bit NormalFloat quantization, double quantization of quantization constants, and paged optimizers for memory spikes. The result: you can fine-tune a 7B model on a $350 GPU. This democratized LLM fine-tuning.

Other PEFT Methods

Adapters

  • Insert small bottleneck layers between transformer layers
  • Structure: down-project → nonlinearity → up-project
  • Each adapter: only ~0.5–8% of model params
  • Con: adds latency (extra layers at inference)

Prefix Tuning

  • Prepend learnable "virtual tokens" to each layer's input
  • Only the prefix embeddings are trained
  • Typically 10–200 prefix tokens
  • Con: reduces effective context length

Houlsby et al. (2019). "Parameter-Efficient Transfer Learning"
Li & Liang (2021). "Prefix-Tuning"

Let me briefly mention two other PEFT approaches. Adapters were one of the first PEFT methods: small bottleneck modules inserted into each transformer layer. They work well but add inference latency because you have extra layers to compute through. Prefix tuning is more elegant: you prepend learnable vectors to the key-value pairs at each attention layer. No architectural changes needed. However, it uses up part of your context window and can be harder to tune. In practice, LoRA won out because it combines the best of both: no inference latency and easy to use.

PEFT Methods Comparison

Method Trainable Params Inference Latency Memory Performance Popularity
Full Fine-tuning 100% Baseline Very High Best Legacy
LoRA 0.1–1% None (merge) Low Near full Most used
QLoRA 0.1–1% None (merge) Very Low Near full Very popular
Adapters 0.5–8% +10–30% Medium Good Moderate
Prefix Tuning 0.1% Slight Low Good* Niche

Recommendation: Start with LoRA (or QLoRA if GPU-constrained). It's the best default.

This is your reference table. If you remember one thing from this session, let it be: start with LoRA or QLoRA. LoRA has won the PEFT competition because it adds zero inference latency, requires very few parameters, and matches full fine-tuning performance. QLoRA if you're working with limited GPU memory. Adapters and prefix tuning have their niches but are less commonly used in practice today.

Practical Fine-tuning

Hugging Face tools and code

Now let's get practical. How do you actually fine-tune a model? The Hugging Face ecosystem makes it remarkably straightforward.

pip install transformers peft trl datasets bitsandbytes
This is the modern fine-tuning stack. Transformers provides the models and training infrastructure. PEFT provides the parameter-efficient methods. TRL (Transformer Reinforcement Learning) provides the SFTTrainer specifically designed for instruction tuning. Datasets handles data loading. And bitsandbytes provides the quantization needed for QLoRA. One pip install and you have everything.

LoRA in Practice: Configuration

from peft import LoraConfig, get_peft_model, TaskType
from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM

# Load pre-trained model
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    "meta-llama/Llama-2-7b-hf",
    device_map="auto"
)

# Configure LoRA
lora_config = LoraConfig(
    task_type=TaskType.CAUSAL_LM,
    r=8,                        # Rank: higher = more expressive, more params
    lora_alpha=32,              # Scaling factor (alpha/r applied to updates)
    lora_dropout=0.05,          # Dropout for regularization
    target_modules=[            # Which layers to apply LoRA to
        "q_proj", "v_proj",     # Attention query and value projections
        "k_proj", "o_proj",     # Attention key and output projections
    ],
)

# Wrap model with LoRA
model = get_peft_model(model, lora_config)
model.print_trainable_parameters()
# trainable params: 4,194,304 || all params: 6,742,609,920 || trainable%: 0.0622
Here's actual code you can run. The key parameters: r is the rank -- start with 8, increase to 16 or 32 if performance isn't good enough. lora_alpha is a scaling factor -- a common rule of thumb is to set it to 2 times r. target_modules specifies which weight matrices get LoRA applied -- typically the attention projections. The last line shows the magic: only 0.06% of parameters are trainable. That's 4 million out of 6.7 billion.

LoRA in Practice: Training

from trl import SFTTrainer, SFTConfig
from datasets import load_dataset

# Load instruction dataset
dataset = load_dataset("databricks/databricks-dolly-15k", split="train")

# Configure training
training_config = SFTConfig(
    output_dir="./lora-llama2",
    num_train_epochs=3,
    per_device_train_batch_size=4,
    gradient_accumulation_steps=4,   # Effective batch size: 4 * 4 = 16
    learning_rate=2e-4,              # Higher than full fine-tuning
    fp16=True,                       # Mixed precision
    logging_steps=10,
    save_strategy="epoch",
)

# Train with SFTTrainer (handles formatting, packing, etc.)
trainer = SFTTrainer(
    model=model,
    train_dataset=dataset,
    args=training_config,
)

trainer.train()

# Save only the LoRA adapter (~16 MB)
model.save_pretrained("./lora-llama2-adapter")
And here's the training code. The SFTTrainer from TRL handles all the instruction formatting for you. Notice the learning rate is 2e-4, which is higher than typical full fine-tuning because we're only updating a small number of parameters. The final save_pretrained call only saves the LoRA adapter weights -- about 16MB instead of 14GB. To use the model later, you load the base model and the adapter separately, then merge them.

QLoRA: Quantized Fine-tuning

from transformers import AutoModelForCausalLM, BitsAndBytesConfig

# 4-bit quantization config
bnb_config = BitsAndBytesConfig(
    load_in_4bit=True,
    bnb_4bit_quant_type="nf4",          # NormalFloat4 quantization
    bnb_4bit_compute_dtype="bfloat16",   # Compute in BF16
    bnb_4bit_use_double_quant=True,      # Quantize the quantization constants
)

# Load model in 4-bit
model = AutoModelForCausalLM.from_pretrained(
    "meta-llama/Llama-2-7b-hf",
    quantization_config=bnb_config,
    device_map="auto",
)

# Apply LoRA on top (same config as before)
model = get_peft_model(model, lora_config)

# Now fine-tune with ~6GB GPU memory instead of ~112GB!

Only 3 extra lines to go from LoRA to QLoRA

The jump from LoRA to QLoRA is remarkably simple. You just add the BitsAndBytesConfig when loading the model. Three key settings: load_in_4bit enables 4-bit quantization, nf4 is the NormalFloat4 format optimized for normally distributed weights, and double quantization further reduces memory by quantizing the quantization constants themselves. Everything else stays the same. The model now fits in about 3.5GB instead of 14GB, and with LoRA adapters and training overhead, total memory is around 6GB.

Practical Fine-tuning Tips

  1. Data quality > data quantity
    1,000 high-quality examples often beats 100,000 noisy ones
  2. Start with LoRA r=8, increase if needed
    r=8 for simple tasks, r=16–64 for complex tasks
  3. Use a validation set to detect overfitting early
    Fine-tuning on small datasets overfits fast
  4. Evaluate on real use cases, not just benchmarks
    Benchmark performance doesn't always transfer
  5. Version your adapters like code
    They're small enough to commit to Git (16 MB)
These tips come from real-world experience. Data quality matters far more than quantity -- garbage in, garbage out. LoRA rank 8 is a solid default; only increase if your evaluation shows the model isn't capturing enough of the task. Overfitting happens quickly with small datasets: always hold out data for validation. Evaluate with actual users or realistic test cases, not just academic benchmarks. And since LoRA adapters are tiny, you can and should version them with Git.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Fine-tuning when prompting would work
    Always try prompting first. Seriously.
  • Using low-quality training data
    The model will learn your mistakes too
  • Training too many epochs
    1–3 epochs is usually enough; more leads to overfitting
  • Ignoring the base model choice
    A better base model = better fine-tuned model. Start with the best you can afford.
  • Not evaluating properly
    Manual evaluation by domain experts is essential
Let me share the most common mistakes I've seen. Number one is the most frequent: engineers jump to fine-tuning before trying prompting. Often, a well-crafted system prompt with examples solves the problem. Number two: bad training data produces a bad model, sometimes worse than the base model. Training too many epochs is subtle: the model starts memorizing your training examples instead of learning general patterns. Base model selection matters a lot -- a fine-tuned Llama-3 will almost always beat a fine-tuned Llama-2. And finally, you must evaluate rigorously -- have domain experts actually use the model.

When to Fine-tune?

Fine-tuning vs prompting vs RAG

This is the most important strategic question. Fine-tuning is powerful, but it's not always the right answer. Let's build a decision framework.

Three Ways to Customize LLMs

Prompting

Easiest. No training.

Write instructions + examples in the prompt.

Cost: $0
Time: minutes

RAG

Add knowledge. No training.

Retrieve relevant docs and inject into prompt.

Cost: low
Time: days

Fine-tuning

Change behavior. Requires training.

Modify model weights with task-specific data.

Cost: moderate
Time: hours–days

Rule of thumb: try prompting first, then RAG, then fine-tuning

This is the practical decision framework. Always start with the simplest approach. Prompting is free and takes minutes. RAG adds your own knowledge base without any training. Fine-tuning is the nuclear option -- use it when you need to change the model's fundamental behavior, not just give it information. A common mistake is jumping to fine-tuning when RAG would suffice.

Choosing the Right Approach

You need to... Best approach Why
Answer questions about your docs RAG Knowledge retrieval, not behavior change
Generate a specific output format Prompting (or fine-tune) Few-shot examples often sufficient
Adopt a consistent tone / persona Fine-tuning Deep behavioral change
Work in a specialized domain (legal, medical) Fine-tuning + RAG Domain vocabulary + retrieval
Reduce latency / cost in production Fine-tune smaller model Distill capabilities into efficient model
Keep information up-to-date RAG Fine-tuned knowledge becomes stale
Walk through each row with the students. RAG is best when the problem is knowledge -- the model doesn't know your company's documentation. Prompting handles formatting and simple instructions. Fine-tuning is for when you need to change HOW the model behaves, not WHAT it knows. The combination of fine-tuning plus RAG is common in production: fine-tune for behavior, RAG for knowledge.

This flowchart is your practical guide. Always start from the top: can you solve it with prompting? If yes, stop there. If prompting fails, ask: is the problem that the model doesn't know something? Then RAG. Is the problem that the model doesn't behave the way you want? Then fine-tune. In practice, you'll often combine approaches. Print this slide and pin it to your wall.

Summary

What we learned in this session

Key Takeaways

  1. Fine-tuning adapts pre-trained models to specific tasks
  2. Full fine-tuning is expensive: updates all params, needs many GPUs
  3. Instruction tuning turns base models into helpful assistants
  4. LoRA/QLoRA make fine-tuning accessible: 0.1% of params, 1 consumer GPU

Six key takeaways. The pre-train then fine-tune paradigm. Full fine-tuning is expensive. Instruction tuning is what makes models useful. LoRA democratized fine-tuning. Use the simplest approach that works. And data quality is king. If you remember these six points, you have a solid understanding of fine-tuning.
