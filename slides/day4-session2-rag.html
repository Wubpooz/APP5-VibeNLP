Why RAG?

LLMs have a fundamental limitation

Knowledge cutoff

  • Training data has a fixed date
  • No access to your private data
  • Cannot know what happened yesterday

Hallucination

  • Generates plausible but false answers
  • Confidently cites non-existent sources
  • No way to verify from weights alone

User: What were our Q4 2025 sales?

LLM: Based on publicly available information, your company reported $4.2M in Q4...

Completely made up. The LLM has never seen your internal data.

Two core problems. First: knowledge cutoff. GPT-4, Claude, etc. were all trained on data up to a certain date. They don't know about your company's internal docs, yesterday's news, or your codebase. Second: hallucination. When an LLM doesn't know something, it doesn't say "I don't know" -- it makes something up that sounds plausible. This is dangerous for production applications. RAG solves both of these.

The RAG Idea

"Don't ask the LLM to remember everything.
Let it look things up when needed."

Knowledge Base
↓ relevant docs
User Query

RAG is beautifully simple in concept. Instead of relying on the LLM's memorized knowledge, we first RETRIEVE relevant documents from a knowledge base, then AUGMENT the prompt with those documents, and finally let the LLM GENERATE an answer based on actual evidence. Think of it as giving the LLM an open-book exam instead of a closed-book exam. This paper was published by Lewis et al. at Meta AI in 2020.

The RAG Pipeline

Six steps from document to answer

1
Document Loading — Ingest PDFs, web pages, databases, APIs
2
Chunking — Split documents into smaller passages
3
Embedding — Convert text chunks to vectors
4
Vector Storage — Store embeddings in a vector database
5
Retrieval — Find most similar chunks to user query
6
Generation — LLM produces answer from query + retrieved context

Steps 1-4 happen offline (indexing) • Steps 5-6 happen at query time

Let's break down the full pipeline. There are 6 steps, but they split into two phases. The INDEXING phase (steps 1-4) happens offline when you prepare your knowledge base. The QUERY phase (steps 5-6) happens in real-time when a user asks a question. This distinction is important for understanding performance. Indexing can be slow; query time must be fast.

Embedding Models

Turning text into vectors

Let's dive into the first key component: embeddings. You saw word embeddings on Day 1 with Word2Vec and GloVe. RAG uses a more powerful version: sentence and passage embeddings.

Embeddings Recap

From Day 1 to RAG

Word Embeddings (Day 1)

  • One vector per word
  • Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText
  • "king" → [0.2, -0.1, ...]
  • Fixed regardless of context

Sentence Embeddings (RAG)

  • One vector per sentence/passage
  • Captures full meaning & context
  • "The cat sat on the mat" → [0.3, 0.7, ...]
  • Context-aware (same word, different meaning)

"How to cook pasta?"

● query ● similar ● distant

Quick recap from Day 1. Word embeddings give you one vector per word, but for RAG we need to embed entire passages. Sentence embeddings capture the full semantic meaning of a chunk of text. The key insight: similar meanings map to nearby points in vector space. This is how retrieval works -- we find the stored passages closest to the user's query.

Vector Databases

Where embeddings live

Now that we can create embeddings, we need somewhere to store them and search through them efficiently. Enter vector databases.

What is a Vector Database?

A database optimized for storing and querying high-dimensional vectors

  • Store millions of embedding vectors
  • Index them for fast similarity search
  • Query "find the 5 most similar vectors to this one"
  • Filter by metadata (date, source, category)

Why not just use a list?

  • 1M vectors × 1536 dims = 6 GB
  • Brute-force search = too slow
  • ANN indexes (HNSW, IVF) = fast approximate search

How ANN Search Works

1. Build an index (graph/tree structure)

2. At query time, navigate the index

3. Check only a small subset of vectors

4. Return approximate top-k results

HNSW: ~95-99% recall at 10x-100x speedup

A vector database is purpose-built for storing and searching embedding vectors. You could use a Python list, but that doesn't scale. With a million documents, brute-force comparison takes too long. Vector databases use Approximate Nearest Neighbor algorithms like HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) that trade a tiny bit of accuracy for massive speed improvements. You get 95-99% recall but 100x faster.

Similarity Metrics

How to measure "closeness" in vector space

Cosine Similarity

cos(A, B) = (A · B) / (||A|| × ||B||)

  • Measures angle between vectors
  • Range: [-1, 1] (1 = identical direction)
  • Most common for text embeddings

Dot Product

A · B = ∑ ai × bi

  • Measures angle and magnitude
  • Fast to compute
  • Use with normalized vectors

L2 / Euclidean Distance

d(A, B) = √∑(ai - bi)²

  • Measures straight-line distance
  • Lower = more similar
  • Sensitive to magnitude

Rule of thumb

Use cosine similarity for text.
Most embedding models are trained to optimize it.

Three main ways to measure how similar two vectors are. Cosine similarity is the standard choice for text because it ignores vector magnitude and focuses on direction -- meaning it measures semantic similarity regardless of text length. Dot product is equivalent when vectors are normalized, which most embedding models do. L2 distance works but is less common for text. When in doubt, use cosine similarity.

Chunking Strategies

The art of splitting documents

Now let's talk about one of the most underrated parts of RAG: how you split your documents into chunks. This has a huge impact on retrieval quality.

Chunking Strategies

Fixed-size chunks

[chars 0-500] The history of NLP begins with...
[chars 500-1000] ...machine translation. In 1954...
[chars 1000-1500] ...statistical methods emerged...

Simple but may cut mid-sentence

Sentence-based

NLP began as rule-based systems. Early work focused on syntax.
Statistical methods changed everything. N-grams dominated.

Respects sentence boundaries

Semantic chunking

Topic: History of NLP — rule-based era...
Topic: Statistical NLP — n-grams, HMMs...

Groups by meaning (uses embeddings)

Chunk Size Trade-offs

Too small (100 tokens)

  • Loses context
  • "He said..." — who is "he"?

Too large (2000 tokens)

  • Dilutes relevance with noise
  • Wastes context window

Sweet spot (200-800 tokens)

  • Enough context to be meaningful
  • Focused enough to be relevant
Chunking strategy matters more than most people think. Fixed-size is simplest: just cut every N characters. Problem: you might cut mid-sentence or mid-thought. Sentence-based respects natural boundaries. Semantic chunking is the smartest: it uses embeddings to detect topic shifts and creates chunks that are semantically coherent. For chunk size, 200-800 tokens is the sweet spot for most use cases. Too small and you lose context; too large and you dilute the signal with noise.

Overlap Strategies

Don't lose information at chunk boundaries

Without overlap:

Chunk 1
Chunk 2
Chunk 3

Information at boundaries is lost

With overlap (e.g., 50 tokens):

Chunk 1
Chunk 2
Chunk 3

Overlapping regions preserve boundary context

Typical settings:

Chunk size: 500 tokens • Overlap: 50-100 tokens (10-20%)

More overlap = better boundary coverage, but more storage & compute

Overlap is a simple trick that significantly improves retrieval quality. Without overlap, if important information spans two chunks, it might not match any single chunk well. With overlap, the end of chunk 1 and the beginning of chunk 2 share some text, so boundary information is preserved. 10-20% overlap is a good default. For a 500-token chunk, that's 50-100 tokens of overlap.

Basic RAG Pipeline in Python


from langchain.document_loaders import PyPDFLoader
from langchain.text_splitter import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter
from langchain.embeddings import OpenAIEmbeddings
from langchain.vectorstores import Chroma
from langchain.chat_models import ChatOpenAI
from langchain.chains import RetrievalQA

# Step 1: Load documents
loader = PyPDFLoader("company_handbook.pdf")
docs = loader.load()

# Step 2: Chunk documents
splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(
    chunk_size=500, chunk_overlap=50
)
chunks = splitter.split_documents(docs)

# Step 3-4: Embed and store in vector database
embeddings = OpenAIEmbeddings(model="text-embedding-3-small")
vectorstore = Chroma.from_documents(chunks, embeddings)

# Step 5-6: Retrieve and generate
llm = ChatOpenAI(model="gpt-4o")
qa_chain = RetrievalQA.from_chain_type(
    llm=llm,
    retriever=vectorstore.as_retriever(search_kwargs={"k": 4}),
)

# Ask a question
answer = qa_chain.invoke("What is our vacation policy?")
print(answer["result"])

~25 lines of code for a complete RAG system — this is why LangChain is popular

Here's a complete, working RAG pipeline in about 25 lines of Python using LangChain. Let me walk through it. Load a PDF, chunk it with 500-token chunks and 50-token overlap, embed with OpenAI, store in Chroma, then create a QA chain that retrieves the top 4 chunks and sends them to GPT-4o. That's it. You can literally copy this code and adapt it for your Demo Day project. The RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter tries to split on paragraphs first, then sentences, then words.

What Happens Under the Hood

The prompt that RAG actually sends to the LLM


System: Answer the question based only on the following context.
If you cannot answer from the context, say "I don't know."

Context:
---
[Chunk 1] "Employees receive 25 days of paid vacation per year.
Vacation days must be approved by your manager at least 2 weeks
in advance..."

[Chunk 2] "Unused vacation days can be carried over to the next
year, up to a maximum of 5 days. Days beyond 5 are forfeited
on January 1st..."

[Chunk 3] "New employees receive a prorated vacation allowance
based on their start date. The formula is: 25 × (remaining
months / 12)..."
---

User: What is our vacation policy?

Key insight: The LLM is just doing reading comprehension, not recalling from memory

This is why RAG reduces hallucination — the answer is right there in the prompt

This is what RAG actually does under the hood. The retrieved chunks get stuffed directly into the prompt as context. The system prompt tells the LLM to ONLY answer from the provided context. So the LLM is essentially doing a reading comprehension task, not a memory recall task. This is fundamentally why RAG reduces hallucination. The answer is right there in the text. The LLM just needs to extract and synthesize it. This "context stuffing" approach is sometimes called the "stuff" method -- simple but effective.

RAG Architecture Patterns

From naive to advanced

Now that you understand the basic pipeline, let's look at how RAG architectures evolve from simple to sophisticated. There are three main patterns you should know about.

Three RAG Patterns

Naive RAG

Query
→
Retrieve
→
Generate
  • Simple retrieve + generate
  • What we just coded
  • Works for 80% of use cases

Limits: bad queries, irrelevant retrieval

Advanced RAG

Query
→
Rewrite
→
Retrieve
→
Re-rank
→
Generate
  • Query expansion: rewrite/decompose query
  • Re-ranking: reorder by relevance
  • Hybrid search: vector + BM25

Modular RAG

Router → Retriever(s) → Filter → Compress → Generate
  • Routing: choose retriever by query type
  • Multi-step: iterative retrieval
  • Agents: decide when to retrieve
Three patterns. Naive RAG is what we coded: simple retrieve and generate. It works surprisingly well for most cases. Advanced RAG adds pre-retrieval steps like query rewriting (use the LLM to improve the search query) and post-retrieval steps like re-ranking (use a cross-encoder to re-score results). Hybrid search combines vector similarity with traditional keyword search (BM25). Modular RAG is the most flexible: you build a pipeline of components that can route, filter, compress, and iteratively retrieve. For Demo Day, start with Naive RAG and add Advanced techniques if needed.

Advanced RAG Techniques

Query Expansion

Original: "vacation days"

↓ LLM rewrites ↓

Expanded: "paid time off policy", "annual leave allowance", "holiday entitlement"

More queries = better recall

Re-ranking

1. Retrieve top-20 candidates (fast)

2. Cross-encoder scores each (query, doc) pair

3. Return top-5 re-ranked results

Much more accurate, slight latency cost

Hybrid Search

Vector search — semantic meaning

+ BM25 keyword search — exact terms

= Combined ranking (RRF)

Best of both worlds

Contextual Compression

Retrieved chunk: 500 tokens (lots of noise)

↓ LLM extracts relevant part ↓

Compressed: 80 tokens (focused)

Less noise in the prompt

Four advanced techniques worth knowing. Query expansion uses the LLM to generate multiple reformulations of the query, then retrieves for all of them and merges results. Hybrid search combines semantic vector search with traditional BM25 keyword search -- this catches cases where the user uses a specific term that vector search might miss. Re-ranking uses a more expensive cross-encoder model to re-score the top results. Contextual compression uses an LLM to extract just the relevant sentences from each chunk, reducing noise. Start simple, add these only when your basic RAG isn't performing well enough.

Three main frameworks. LangChain is the most popular -- it has the largest community and the most integrations. It's great for prototyping but can feel over-abstracted for production. LlamaIndex was built specifically for RAG and has better indexing and retrieval abstractions. Haystack by deepset is pipeline-based and has excellent evaluation tools. For your Demo Day projects, I'd recommend LangChain because there are the most tutorials and Stack Overflow answers. But all three are solid.

When to Use What?

Decision framework

Prompting — Start here

Use when: task is well-defined, data fits in context window, no specialized knowledge needed

Examples: summarization, translation, code generation, structured output

RAG — When you need external knowledge

Use when: data is too large for context, changes frequently, needs to be verifiable, private/proprietary

Examples: Q&A over docs, customer support, research assistant, code search

Fine-tuning — When you need behavior change

Use when: specific output format/style, domain-specific language, consistent persona, latency-critical

Examples: medical report generation, legal document drafting, custom chatbot tone

These are not mutually exclusive — you can combine RAG + fine-tuning + prompting

This is the decision framework you should use for your projects. Always start with prompting -- it's cheapest and fastest to iterate. If the LLM needs access to knowledge it doesn't have, add RAG. If you need to change HOW the LLM behaves (its style, format, or domain expertise), consider fine-tuning. And remember: these stack. You can fine-tune a model AND use RAG AND use good prompting. For most Demo Day projects, RAG + good prompting will be the sweet spot.

Comparison at a Glance

Prompting RAG Fine-tuning
Setup time Minutes Hours Days
Cost Low (API only) Medium (embed + store + API) High (training compute)
Data freshness N/A Real-time possible Frozen at training time
Hallucination High risk Low (grounded) Medium

Here's the side-by-side comparison. Notice that RAG uniquely offers real-time data freshness and high transparency (you can show users exactly which documents the answer came from). Fine-tuning is best when you need to change the model's behavior fundamentally. For your Demo Day, the sweet spot is usually RAG because you can build impressive demos quickly and your answers are grounded in real data.

RAG Evaluation

How do you know if your RAG system is working?

Context Relevance — Are the retrieved documents actually relevant to the query?

Measures retrieval quality. If you retrieve garbage, the answer will be garbage.

Answer Faithfulness — Is the answer supported by the retrieved context?

Detects hallucination. The answer should only contain claims from the context.

Answer Relevance — Does the answer actually address the user's question?

End-to-end quality. A faithful answer to the wrong question is still bad.

Tools for evaluation: RAGAS (automated metrics) • LangSmith (tracing & debugging) • Phoenix (observability) • DeepEval (unit tests for LLMs)

Evaluation is critical. Three metrics form the RAG triad. Context relevance measures retrieval quality -- are you finding the right documents? Answer faithfulness checks for hallucination -- is the LLM making up stuff not in the context? Answer relevance checks end-to-end quality -- does the answer address the question? RAGAS is a great open-source framework that automates all three metrics using LLM-as-judge. LangSmith lets you trace every step of your pipeline. For Demo Day, at minimum test with 20-30 question-answer pairs and compute these metrics.
