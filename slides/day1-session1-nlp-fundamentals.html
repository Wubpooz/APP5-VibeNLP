NLP: The Intersection

Artificial Intelligence Computer Science Linguistics NLP

NLU (Understanding) + NLG (Generation) = NLP

NLP sits at the intersection of three fields. NLU is about comprehension, NLG is about producing language. Together they form NLP.

Why Language is Hard

  • Ambiguity
    "I saw the man with the telescope" — who has the telescope?
  • Context dependence
    "bank" — river bank? financial bank?
  • Implicit knowledge
    "The trophy doesn't fit in the suitcase because it is too big" — what is "it"?
  • Sarcasm & irony
    "Oh great, another meeting" — positive or negative?
The Winograd Schema (trophy/suitcase) is a classic AI challenge. Ask students: Can you think of other ambiguous sentences?

Levels of Linguistic Analysis

Pragmatics
Semantics
Syntax
Morphology
Phonology
Sounds → Word structure → Sentence structure → Meaning → Context & intent
Build from concrete (bottom) to abstract (top). Example: "unbelievable" — phonology (sounds), morphology (un-believe-able), syntax (adjective role), semantics (incredulity), pragmatics (admiration or frustration?).

Core NLP Tasks: Understanding

  • Tokenization — "I love NLP" → ["I", "love", "NLP"]
  • POS Tagging — "I/PRP love/VBP NLP/NNP"
  • Named Entity Recognition — "[Paris]LOC in [March]DATE"
  • Parsing — syntactic structure (dependency trees)
  • Sentiment Analysis — "Great movie!" → Positive
These are the classic "understanding" tasks. Most were the focus of NLP for decades before generation became the hot topic.

Core NLP Tasks: Generation

  • Machine Translation — "Bonjour le monde" → "Hello world"
  • Text Summarization — long article → key points
  • Question Answering — context + question → answer
  • Text Generation — prompt → full text (ChatGPT, Claude)
  • Dialogue Systems — multi-turn conversation
Generation has become the dominant paradigm thanks to LLMs. Note how ChatGPT and Claude do ALL of these tasks with a single model.

This is the intellectual backbone of today's lecture. Each era represents a fundamental shift in how we approach language.

Era 1: Rule-Based NLP

1950s — 1990s

  • Hand-crafted rules & grammars
  • Linguists write rules; computers follow
  • Interpretable, precise for narrow domains
  • Doesn't scale — language is too complex
ELIZA (1966)
User: I feel sad today
ELIZA: Why do you feel sad today?
User: My mother doesn't like me
ELIZA: Tell me more about your family
ELIZA used simple pattern matching and substitution — no real understanding. But it convinced many people it was "intelligent." First chatbot. SHRDLU (1970) could understand commands about blocks in a virtual world.

Era 2: Statistical NLP

1990s — 2012

  • Paradigm shift: "Let the data decide"
  • Bag-of-Words — text as word frequency counts
  • TF-IDF — importance = frequency × rarity
  • n-gram models — P(word | previous n-1 words)
  • HMMs, CRFs — sequence labeling (POS, NER)
  • IBM Models — statistical machine translation
The key insight: instead of encoding linguistic rules, learn patterns from data. Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF are still used today as baselines. This era laid the groundwork for everything that followed.
"Every time I fire a linguist, the performance of the speech recognizer goes up."

— Fred Jelinek, IBM (1988)

The shift from modeling language to modeling data
enabled the next 30 years of progress.

This quote is controversial but captures the paradigm shift perfectly. Ask students: Was Jelinek right? Is linguistics obsolete in the age of LLMs? The answer is nuanced — linguistic insights still inform model design.

Era 3: Neural NLP

2013 — 2017

  • Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) — words as vectors
  • RNNs & LSTMs — modeling sequences
  • Seq2Seq (Sutskever et al., 2014) — neural machine translation
  • Attention (Bahdanau et al., 2015) — learning where to look

We will dive deep into Word2Vec in Session 2 today.

Word2Vec was the breakthrough that made everyone pay attention. The key innovation: dense, continuous representations learned from data. Attention mechanism is the direct ancestor of Transformers.

The Representation Revolution

Before: One-Hot

cat  = [0, 0, 1, 0, 0, ...]
dog  = [0, 0, 0, 1, 0, ...]
fish = [0, 0, 0, 0, 1, ...]

similarity(cat, dog) = 0

No notion of similarity

After: Dense Vectors

cat  = [0.2, -0.4, 0.7, ...]
dog  = [0.3, -0.3, 0.8, ...]
fish = [0.8,  0.1, 0.2, ...]

similarity(cat, dog) = 0.95

Similar words are nearby

king − man + woman ≈ queen

This is THE single most important idea in modern NLP. The king-queen analogy captivated the entire field and the public. Session 2 will explore this in depth with hands-on exercises.

Scaling Laws: The Bitter Lesson

  • Chinchilla scaling laws: performance improves predictably with compute + data
  • More parameters + more data = better performance
  • This is why companies invest $500B+
"The biggest lesson that can be read from 70 years of AI research is that general methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective."

— Rich Sutton, "The Bitter Lesson" (2019)

Connect back to Slide 5 — the massive investment makes sense IF scaling laws hold. The "bitter" part: hand-crafted features always lose to brute-force computation eventually. But there's debate: are we hitting diminishing returns? Inference-time scaling is the new frontier.

Step 1: Tokenization

Breaking text into subword units

"unbelievable" → ["un", "believ", "able"]
"ChatGPT"      → ["Chat", "G", "PT"]
"NLP"          → ["N", "LP"]
  • BPE (Byte Pair Encoding) — most common approach
  • Balances character-level and word-level granularity
  • Different models use different tokenizers — and it matters
Ask students: How would you tokenize "ChatGPT"? Different models give different answers. Tokenization seems simple but has huge downstream effects.

Step 2: Embeddings

Converting tokens to dense vectors

  • Each token becomes a point in high-dimensional space
  • Learned during training — not hand-designed
  • Similar meanings → similar vectors

Session 2 today goes deep on this

Word2Vec, GloVe, and hands-on with vector operations

Brief preview — don't go too deep here. The key idea: representation learning. Vectors capture meaning.

Step 3: The Transformer

The engine behind modern NLP

  • Self-attention: every token looks at every other token
  • Analogy: reading a sentence while being aware of the entire paragraph
  • Stacked layers of Attention + Feed-Forward networks

Step 4: Pre-training & Fine-tuning

Pre-training

Learn general language knowledge from massive corpora

  • MLM (BERT): predict masked words
  • CLM (GPT): predict the next word

Fine-tuning

Adapt to specific tasks with smaller datasets

  • Task-specific training
  • LoRA, adapters (PEFT)

Analogy: pre-training = going to school  |  fine-tuning = specializing
