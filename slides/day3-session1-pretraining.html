Part 1

What Is Pre-training and Why Does It Matter?

Let's start with the fundamental question: why pre-train at all? This is the most important conceptual point of the session.

Before Pre-training

The Old Way

  • Collect labeled data for your task
  • Train a model from scratch
  • Need 10K–100K+ labeled examples
  • Model knows nothing about language

The New Way

  • Pre-train on massive unlabeled text
  • Fine-tune on your specific task
  • Need only 100–1K labeled examples
  • Model already understands language
Before 2018, if you wanted to do sentiment analysis, you needed thousands of labeled movie reviews. The model started as a blank slate. After pre-training, the model already knows grammar, facts, reasoning patterns. You just need a small number of examples to teach it YOUR specific task. This is the transfer learning revolution.

Transfer Learning

Pre-training is like going to school for 20 years. Fine-tuning is like your first week at a new job.
Pre-train
Learn language from
billions of words
→
Fine-tune
Adapt to specific
task or domain
→
Deploy
Use on real
user queries
This analogy helps a lot. Think of pre-training as general education. The model reads the internet and learns how language works, learns facts about the world, learns reasoning patterns. Fine-tuning is like specialized training for a specific job. It is much faster because you already have a strong foundation. This is exactly the concept from Howard and Ruder's ULMFiT (2018) and was dramatically scaled up by BERT and GPT.

Why Does Pre-training Work?

  • Self-supervised learning: Labels come for free from the text itself
    No human annotation needed — the next word IS the label
  • Practically infinite data: The internet has trillions of words
    No bottleneck of expensive human labeling
  • Language encodes knowledge: Predicting text forces learning of
    Grammar, facts, reasoning, common sense, even code
  • Universal representations: Same pre-trained model works for many tasks
    Sentiment, QA, translation, summarization, coding...
The key insight is SELF-SUPERVISED learning. You don't need humans to label data. The text itself provides the supervision signal. If you take a sentence and hide a word, predicting that word forces the model to understand syntax, semantics, and world knowledge. This is what makes pre-training scalable to trillions of tokens.

Language Modeling: The Pre-training Objective

Now let's get specific. What exactly does the model learn during pre-training? There are two main approaches, and they lead to fundamentally different models.

Two Approaches to Language Modeling

Masked LM (MLM)

Hide some words,
predict them from context

🎭

BERT (2018)

Bidirectional

Causal LM (CLM)

Read left-to-right,
predict the next word

➡

GPT (2018)

Unidirectional (autoregressive)

Both approaches came out around the same time (2018) and represent fundamentally different philosophies. BERT says: look at ALL the context around a word. GPT says: read left to right, one word at a time. Both are valid, but they lead to models with very different strengths. Let's dive into each.

Masked Language Modeling (MLM)

BERT's Approach — Devlin et al., 2018

Input sentence:

The
cat
sat
on
the
warm
mat

Randomly mask 15% of tokens:

The
[MASK]
sat
on
the
[MASK]
mat

Model predicts: [MASK] → "cat", [MASK] → "warm"

Here is the core idea of BERT. Take a sentence, randomly pick 15% of the tokens, replace them with a special MASK token, and ask the model to predict what was there. The key: the model can look at BOTH sides (left and right context) to make its prediction. "The ___ sat on the ___ mat" - you need context from both directions to figure out these are "cat" and "warm".

The 80/10/10 Masking Strategy

Of the 15% selected tokens:

80%
The
[MASK]
sat
Replace with [MASK] token
10%
The
dog
sat
Replace with random word
10%
The
cat
sat
Keep original word (unchanged)

Why? If always [MASK], the model learns to only work when it sees [MASK].
Random + unchanged tokens force the model to maintain good representations for ALL tokens.

This is a clever trick. If you always use MASK, the model learns a shortcut: it only needs to pay attention when it sees the MASK token. By sometimes using random words and sometimes keeping the original, the model must maintain useful representations for every position, because any token MIGHT need to be predicted. The 10% random also prevents the model from just copying the input.

Next Sentence Prediction (NSP)

BERT's second pre-training objective:

IsNext (50%)
[CLS] The cat sat on the mat [SEP] It was a warm afternoon [SEP]
NotNext (50%)
[CLS] The cat sat on the mat [SEP] Stock prices rose sharply [SEP]
  • Helps the model understand inter-sentence relationships
  • Later work (RoBERTa, 2019) showed NSP may not be necessary
  • Replaced by better objectives like Sentence Order Prediction (ALBERT)
BERT also has a second task: given two sentences, predict whether the second sentence actually follows the first in the original document. This was meant to help with tasks like question answering and natural language inference. However, later research by Liu et al. in RoBERTa showed that NSP doesn't actually help much. The MLM objective alone is powerful enough. ALBERT replaced it with Sentence Order Prediction which worked better.

MLM Training Objective

Masked Language Model Loss
LMLM = − ∑i ∈ masked log P(xi | x\masked ; θ)
  • xi = the original token at masked position i
  • x\masked = all unmasked tokens (both left AND right context)
  • θ = model parameters
  • Key: the model sees bidirectional context

In plain English: maximize the probability of correctly predicting each masked token, given all the surrounding unmasked tokens.

Let's look at the math, which is straightforward. For each masked position, we compute the cross-entropy loss between the model's prediction and the true token. The critical thing to notice: the conditioning is on x_backslash_masked, meaning ALL unmasked tokens. The model looks left AND right. This is what makes BERT bidirectional. Contrast this with what GPT does, which we'll see next.

BERT uses only the Transformer ENCODER (remember, the original Transformer had both encoder and decoder). Because BERT uses the encoder, attention is bidirectional - each token attends to all other tokens. BERT-Base has 12 layers, 768 hidden size, 12 attention heads. BERT-Large has 24 layers, 1024 hidden size, 16 heads. Trained on BookCorpus and English Wikipedia, about 3.3 billion words total.

Part 3

Causal Language Modeling: GPT's Approach

Now let's look at the other approach. GPT came out slightly before BERT (GPT-1 in June 2018, BERT in October 2018), but the GPT approach became the dominant paradigm for large language models.

Causal Language Modeling (CLM)

The GPT Approach — Radford et al., 2018

Predict the next token, reading left-to-right:

The
→
cat
→
sat
→
on
→
the
→
?

At each position, predict the next token using only previous tokens:

"The" → cat
"The cat" → sat
"The cat sat" → on
GPT uses causal language modeling, also called autoregressive language modeling. The idea is simple: read a sequence of tokens from left to right, and at each step, predict the next token. The model can only look at what came BEFORE, never ahead. This is like reading a book one word at a time and constantly guessing what comes next. This constraint is enforced by a causal attention mask in the Transformer.

CLM Training Objective

Causal Language Model Loss
LCLM = − ∑t=1T log P(xt | x1, x2, ..., xt−1 ; θ)
  • xt = token at position t
  • Conditioning on x1 ... xt−1 only = left context
  • Every token is a training example (not just 15%)
  • Natural for text generation: just keep predicting next tokens

In plain English: maximize the probability of each token given everything that came before it.

Compare this formula to BERT's. The key difference: the conditioning is only on x_1 through x_{t-1}. The model can only look LEFT. Also notice: every token is a training signal, not just 15%. This means CLM is more training-efficient per token. And crucially, this objective directly corresponds to text generation: to generate text, you just keep predicting the next token, one at a time. This is why GPT-style models are so good at generation.

Autoregressive Generation

CLM naturally enables text generation:

Prompt: "The capital of France is"

Step 1: P(next | "The capital of France is") → Paris

Step 2: P(next | "The capital of France is Paris") → ,

Step 3: P(next | "The capital of France is Paris,") → a

Step 4: P(next | "The capital of France is Paris, a") → city

... and so on until end-of-sequence token

This is exactly how ChatGPT, Claude, and every modern LLM generates text. One token at a time, each conditioned on everything before it.

This is how every LLM chatbot works: it produces text one token at a time. Each new token is sampled from the probability distribution the model outputs. The model feeds each generated token back into itself to produce the next one. That streaming effect you see when ChatGPT or Claude is typing? That is literally the model generating tokens one by one. This is called autoregressive generation.

GPT: Architecture Overview

Output: Predict next token at each position
↑
Transformer Decoder × N layers
Causal (masked) self-attention — can only look left
↑
Token Embeddings + Position Embeddings
↑
Input: "The cat sat on the"

Uses the Transformer Decoder (not Encoder).
The causal attention mask ensures each position can only attend to earlier positions.

GPT uses the Transformer Decoder, unlike BERT which uses the Encoder. The key difference is the causal attention mask: a triangular mask that prevents each position from seeing future tokens. This is the same masked self-attention from the original Transformer's decoder, but without the cross-attention to an encoder. Sometimes called "decoder-only" architecture.

MLM vs CLM: Comparison

MLM (BERT) CLM (GPT)
Direction Bidirectional Left-to-right only
Architecture Encoder Decoder
Training signal 15% of tokens 100% of tokens
Best for Understanding tasks Generation tasks
Example uses Classification, NER, QA extraction Text generation, chatbots, coding
Scaling winner Plateaued at ~1B Scaled to 1T+
Let's compare directly. BERT sees both directions, making it better at understanding. GPT sees only left context, making it natural for generation. BERT trains on only 15% of tokens per example; GPT on all tokens. In practice, CLM won the scaling race: all models above about 1 billion parameters today use CLM or variants of it. BERT-style models are still excellent for classification and extraction tasks where you need deep bidirectional understanding.

GPT is like finishing someone's sentence: you only hear what they've said so far and predict what comes next. Both are useful skills, but generation (GPT's strength) turned out to be the more powerful paradigm at scale.

Part 4

Scaling Laws: Bigger Is (Predictably) Better

One of the most remarkable discoveries in deep learning: the performance of language models follows predictable mathematical laws as you scale up. Let's look at what Kaplan et al. and the Chinchilla team discovered.

Scaling Laws

Kaplan et al., 2020 (OpenAI)

Loss follows power laws in three variables:

N

Model Size

Number of parameters

L(N) ∝ N−0.076
D

Dataset Size

Number of tokens

L(D) ∝ D−0.095
C

Compute

FLOPs used for training

L(C) ∝ C−0.050

These power laws hold over many orders of magnitude. Loss decreases smoothly and predictably as you increase N, D, or C — no sudden jumps or plateaus.

This was a landmark paper. They trained hundreds of models of different sizes and discovered that loss follows very clean power laws. Double the parameters, and the loss decreases by a predictable amount. Double the data, same thing. Double the compute, same thing. This means we can PREDICT how well a model will perform before training it. This is what gave labs the confidence to invest hundreds of millions of dollars in training runs. You can estimate the return before you spend the money.

Chinchilla: Compute-Optimal Training

Hoffmann et al., 2022 (DeepMind)

Key finding:

Chinchilla Optimal Ratio
Tokens ≈ 20 × Parameters
  • Most models were undertrained
  • GPT-3 (175B params) was trained on 300B tokens
  • Chinchilla optimal: should have used 3.5T tokens

Example:

Gopher Chinchilla
Params 280B 70B
Tokens 300B 1.4T
Compute Same!
Result Chinchilla wins
Chinchilla was a game-changer. DeepMind showed that for a given compute budget, you should balance model size and data. Most earlier models were too big for their training data. Chinchilla used 4x fewer parameters than Gopher but 4.7x more data, with the same total compute budget, and outperformed Gopher on nearly every benchmark. The rule of thumb: 20 tokens per parameter. This directly influenced the design of Llama, Mistral, and other efficient models.

Part 5

Pre-training Data and Cost

Let's talk about what these models actually train on and how much it costs. This is where the economics of AI become very concrete.

Pre-training Data Sources

Common Crawl
~3T tokens

Web pages from the entire internet. Massive but noisy.

The Pile
825 GB

Curated mix: books, arXiv, GitHub, StackExchange, Wikipedia

RedPajama
1.2T tokens

Open reproduction of LLaMA training data

Wikipedia
~4B tokens

High-quality, factual, multi-lingual encyclopedia

Books / BookCorpus
~1B tokens

Long-form coherent text, narrative structure

Code (GitHub)
~500B tokens

Source code in many languages. Helps with reasoning.

Modern models use carefully curated mixtures. Llama 3 used 15T+ tokens after extensive filtering and deduplication.

Data is the fuel of pre-training. Common Crawl is the biggest source but very noisy: ads, spam, duplicated content. That's why data curation is critical. The Pile was one of the first open curated datasets. RedPajama aimed to reproduce Meta's LLaMA data. An interesting finding: including CODE in training data helps with reasoning even on non-code tasks. Code is highly structured and logical, which seems to help the model learn reasoning patterns. Data quality matters enormously: garbage in, garbage out.

Let's make this concrete. BERT was cheap to train by today's standards. GPT-3 cost about 4.6 million dollars, which was shocking at the time. GPT-4 is estimated at around 100 million dollars just for compute, not including research salaries, data, etc. Llama 3's 405B model used over 16,000 H100 GPUs for months. This is why pre-training is concentrated among a handful of well-funded labs. If your training run crashes halfway through, you might have just lost $50M. This also explains why there is so much interest in more efficient training methods.

Let me walk through the key milestones. BERT (2018) showed that pre-training works amazingly well. GPT-2 (2019) showed that scaling CLM produces eerily fluent text. T5 (Google, 2019) tried a unified text-to-text framework with encoder-decoder. GPT-3 (2020) was the watershed moment: 175B parameters, in-context learning, few-shot capabilities. PaLM (Google, 2022) scaled to 540B. LLaMA (Meta, 2023) showed you can get great results with smaller, more efficiently trained models. GPT-4 (2023) set a new bar. Llama 3 (2024) pushed open models further. Claude and Gemini represent the current state of the art. Notice: everything after GPT-2 uses CLM or encoder-decoder. Pure MLM stopped scaling.

What Do Pre-trained Models Actually Learn?

Linguistic Knowledge

  • Syntax and grammar
  • Word meanings and relations
  • Coreference (who "he" refers to)
  • Discourse structure

World Knowledge

  • Facts ("Paris is the capital of France")
  • Common sense reasoning
  • Mathematical reasoning
  • Coding patterns
The surprising insight

Just predicting the next word, at sufficient scale, produces models that can reason, translate, code, and answer questions.

This is the most remarkable thing about pre-training. All you teach the model is to predict text, and it spontaneously develops abilities that seem like understanding. Probing studies have shown that BERT's internal representations encode syntax trees, part-of-speech information, and semantic relationships, even though nobody explicitly taught it any of these things. The same is true for GPT models but at even larger scale: they learn to do arithmetic, write code, translate languages, all from next-token prediction. This is sometimes called "emergence" - capabilities that appear at scale.
