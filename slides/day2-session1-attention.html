Part 1: Why RNNs Hit a Wall

The problems that attention was invented to solve

Before we can appreciate attention, we need to understand what was broken. Let's go back to RNNs and sequence-to-sequence models.

Before diving into the problems, let's establish what architecture we're talking about. The sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) model, introduced by Sutskever et al. (2014), chains two RNNs. The ENCODER reads the input sentence word by word and distills everything into a single fixed-size context vector — a compressed representation of the entire input. The DECODER starts from that context vector as its initial hidden state and generates the output one word at a time, feeding each predicted word back as input for the next step. This was the dominant architecture for machine translation before attention. But as we'll see, that single context vector is a serious bottleneck.

What this means in practice:

  • The model cannot learn long-range patterns
  • Like trying to shout across a football field — the sound fades

Even LSTMs & GRUs (designed to fix this) struggle beyond ~200 tokens

Vanishing gradients is the most famous RNN problem. When we train with backpropagation through time, the gradient signal must travel backward through every time step. At each step, the gradient gets multiplied by a weight matrix. If those values are less than 1, the gradient shrinks exponentially. After 20-30 steps, the gradient is essentially zero. This means the model cannot learn relationships between words that are far apart. LSTMs and GRUs helped somewhat with their gating mechanisms, but they still struggle with very long sequences.

The second major problem is the information bottleneck. In a standard sequence-to-sequence model, the encoder processes the entire input sentence and produces a single fixed-size vector. This vector is supposed to capture everything about the input. For short sentences, this is fine. But for longer inputs, a fixed-size vector simply cannot hold all the nuances. It is like asking someone to summarize an entire novel in a single tweet. Information inevitably gets lost, especially details from early in the sentence.

This is why training RNNs on long sequences is painfully slow. GPUs have thousands of cores but RNNs can only use them one step at a time.

The third problem is about speed. RNNs are inherently sequential: you cannot compute h3 until you have computed h2, which requires h1. This means you cannot parallelize the computation. Modern GPUs have thousands of cores designed for parallel computation, but RNNs leave most of them idle. For a 1000-word document, you need 1000 sequential steps. This makes training extremely slow. As we will see, attention opens the door to parallel processing of all positions simultaneously.

The Long-Range Dependency Problem

"The cat, which was sitting on the mat in the living room next to the old fireplace that hadn't been used in years, was very happy."

"She told him about the project during the meeting that lasted three hours on a rainy Tuesday afternoon, and then she left."

↑ "she" in the second clause refers back to "She" at the very beginning

Key insight: Language is full of long-range dependencies. A model that forgets early words will make systematic errors.
Let me give you concrete examples of why these RNN limitations matter in real language. In the first example, the verb "was" must agree with "cat", but they are separated by 20 words. An RNN would need to carry the information about "cat" through all those intermediate hidden states. In practice, this information gets diluted or lost. The model might incorrectly use "were" instead. These long-range dependencies are everywhere in natural language, and they are essential for correct translation, summarization, and understanding.

Part 2: The Attention Mechanism

"Don't compress everything — look back at the input!"

Now we get to the breakthrough. The core idea of attention is beautifully simple: instead of forcing all information through a single bottleneck vector, let the decoder LOOK BACK at all the encoder hidden states and decide which ones are relevant for each output word. Let's see how this works.

The Key Intuition Behind Attention

Analogy: Reading an Open Book vs. Memorizing It

Without attention (standard seq2seq): Read the entire book, close it, then write a summary from memory alone.

With attention: Keep the book open! For each sentence you write in the summary, flip back to the relevant pages and re-read them.

"Which input words should I focus on right now?"

The decoder asks this question at every output step

The analogy I like best is the open-book exam vs closed-book exam. Standard seq2seq is like a closed-book exam: read everything, then answer from memory. Attention is like an open-book exam: for each question, you can look back at the relevant material. At each decoding step, the attention mechanism computes a relevance score between the current decoder state and every encoder hidden state. It then creates a weighted sum of encoder states, focusing on the most relevant ones. This is the context vector.

The Math

Step 1: Compute alignment scores

et,i = vT tanh(Wa st-1 + Ua hi)

Step 2: Normalize with softmax

αt,i = softmax(et,i)

Step 3: Compute context vector

ct = Σi αt,i · hi

Bahdanau, Cho & Bengio. "Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate." ICLR 2015 (arXiv 2014)

Bahdanau attention was the first attention mechanism for NLP, published in 2014. It is called "additive" because the alignment score uses addition inside a tanh. Here is how it works step by step: 1. For each encoder hidden state h_i, compute an alignment score e_t,i that measures how well h_i matches the current decoder state s_{t-1}. This uses a small neural network. 2. Apply softmax to get attention weights alpha that sum to 1. 3. Compute a weighted sum of all encoder states, weighted by alpha. This is the context vector. The context vector is then concatenated with the decoder input to help make the next prediction. The key innovation: the decoder can now "look at" all encoder states, not just the last one.

Luong Attention (2015)

Also called "Multiplicative Attention"

Three scoring functions:

Name Formula Intuition
Dot Product et,i = stT hi Simplest: just multiply
General et,i = stT Wa hi Learnable weight matrix
Concat et,i = vT tanh(Wa[st;hi]) Like Bahdanau
Key difference from Bahdanau: Luong uses the current decoder hidden state st (not st-1), and the dot-product variant is much faster to compute.

Luong, Pham & Manning. "Effective Approaches to Attention-based Neural Machine Translation." EMNLP 2015

A year after Bahdanau, Luong et al. proposed simpler alternatives for computing attention scores. The most important one is the dot product: just take the dot product of the decoder state and encoder state. No neural network needed! This is much faster to compute. The "general" variant adds a learnable weight matrix for more expressiveness. The "concat" variant is essentially the same as Bahdanau. The dot product variant turned out to be especially important because it is the basis for the attention used in Transformers. Simple and efficient.

Bahdanau vs. Luong: Side by Side

Both achieve the same goal: let the decoder look at all encoder states

The dot-product approach (Luong) became the foundation for Transformer attention

Here is a clean comparison of the two attention mechanisms. Both solve the same fundamental problem, but they differ in the details. Bahdanau uses a small neural network (additive) to compute scores, while Luong uses a simple dot product (multiplicative). Luong is generally faster. In practice, the dot-product approach won out and became the basis for Transformer attention. The key idea is the same in both: don't rely on a single bottleneck vector. Let the decoder attend to all parts of the input at each step.

Let's pause and check understanding. Attention solves two of the three problems: the bottleneck (decoder can now access all encoder states) and long-range dependencies (direct attention connections mean information doesn't need to travel through many hidden states). But attention does NOT solve the sequential processing problem. The underlying encoder and decoder are still RNNs that must process one step at a time. To solve that, we need self-attention and Transformers, which is exactly where we are headed next.

Part 3: Self-Attention

The breakthrough that made Transformers possible

Now we arrive at the most important concept of the session: self-attention. While Bahdanau and Luong attention let the decoder attend to encoder states, self-attention is about a sequence attending to ITSELF. This is the core mechanism of the Transformer architecture.

Self-attention: Every word in a sentence looks at every other word (including itself) to build a richer representation.
In cross-attention (Bahdanau/Luong), we have two different sequences: the decoder attends to the encoder. Self-attention is a different idea: a single sequence attends to itself. Every word computes attention scores with every other word in the same sentence. This lets each word "look around" at its context to understand its own meaning better. For example, the word "bank" can look at nearby words to determine if it means "river bank" or "financial bank". Self-attention replaces the RNN entirely: no more sequential processing!

Query, Key, Value: The Search Engine Analogy

Analogy: Searching a Library Database

Query (Q): "I'm looking for a book about French cooking" — this is what you're searching for.

Key (K): The title/keywords of each book in the catalog — these are the labels that help you match.

Value (V): The actual content of each book — this is the information you want to retrieve.

Query
matches against
Keys
to retrieve
Values

In self-attention, every word generates its own Q, K, and V vectors.
Each word's Query is matched against all words' Keys to decide how much of each word's Value to collect.

The Query-Key-Value framework is central to understanding attention. Let me explain with a library analogy. When you search a library database: - Your search query (Q) represents what you are looking for. - Each book has keywords/title (K) that the system matches against your query. - The actual book content (V) is what gets returned to you. The matching between Q and K determines relevance. Higher relevance means more of that book's content (V) is returned. In self-attention, every word in the sentence plays all three roles simultaneously. Each word generates a Query (what am I looking for?), a Key (what do I advertise?), and a Value (what information do I provide?). These are computed by multiplying the word's embedding by three learned weight matrices: W_Q, W_K, and W_V.

Computing Q, K, V

For each word embedding xi, compute three vectors:

qi = WQ · xi    (Query)
ki = WK · xi    (Key)
vi = WV · xi    (Value)

WQ, WK, WV are learned weight matrices
They are the same for all positions — shared parameters

Think of it as: each word learns three different representations of itself — one for searching, one for being found, one for providing information.
Here is the concrete computation. We take the word embedding vector x_i and multiply it by three different learned weight matrices to get three different vectors: - Q: what this word is "looking for" in other words - K: what this word "advertises" about itself to other words - V: what this word "provides" when it is attended to These weight matrices are learned during training. The model figures out the best way to decompose each word into these three roles. This is one of the elegant aspects of attention: the same word can be a good match as a Key for one query but not another.

Scaled Dot-Product Attention

The core formula from "Attention Is All You Need"

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( Q KT / √dk ) · V

Step by step:

  1. Dot product: Q KT — compute similarity between each query and all keys
  2. Scale: Divide by √dk — prevent dot products from getting too large
  3. Softmax: Convert scores to probabilities (sum to 1)
  4. Weighted sum: Multiply by V — retrieve information from values

Vaswani et al. "Attention Is All You Need." NeurIPS 2017

This is THE formula. Memorize this if you memorize anything from today. Attention of Q, K, V equals softmax of Q times K-transpose divided by square root of d_k, times V. Let me walk through each step: 1. Q times K-transpose: This computes the dot product between every query and every key. If a query and key point in the same direction, the score is high (they match). 2. Divide by square root of d_k: This is the "scaling" part. Without this, when d_k is large, the dot products become very large, pushing softmax into regions with tiny gradients. The square root scaling keeps the values in a reasonable range. 3. Softmax: Converts raw scores into a probability distribution. The highest-scoring keys get the most weight, and all weights sum to 1. 4. Multiply by V: The weighted combination of Value vectors, weighted by the attention scores. This entire computation is a matrix multiplication, which GPUs can do extremely fast!

Why Divide by √dk?

Without scaling (dk = 512):

Dot products can be huge: [-150, 200, -80, 300]

After softmax: [0.00, 0.00, 0.00, 1.00]

Almost one-hot! Gradients vanish.

With scaling (÷ √512 ≈ 22.6):

Scaled scores: [-6.6, 8.8, -3.5, 13.3]

After softmax: [0.00, 0.01, 0.00, 0.99]

Still peaked, but gradients flow.

Intuition
If two random vectors of dimension dk have elements drawn from a standard normal distribution, their dot product has mean 0 and variance dk. Dividing by √dk brings the variance back to 1, keeping softmax in its "useful" range.
This is a practical detail that matters a lot. When you compute the dot product of two random vectors, the expected magnitude of the result grows with the dimension. For d_k = 512, the dot products can be very large. When you pass large values into softmax, you get outputs very close to 0 or 1, which means almost all the gradient flows to a single position. This makes training unstable. The fix is elegant: divide by the square root of d_k. This normalizes the variance of the dot products back to approximately 1, keeping softmax in the region where it produces useful gradients. This is why it is called SCALED dot-product attention.

Let's trace through a concrete example. In "The cat sat on the mat", let's look at the word "cat". Using its Query vector, it computes attention scores with the Key vectors of all words. Self-Attention Solves All Three RNN Problems

1. No vanishing gradients

Every word has a direct connection to every other word. Gradients flow directly — no long chain of multiplications.

2. No information bottleneck

Each word's representation is built from all words simultaneously. No single vector must encode everything.

3. Fully parallelizable

All attention scores can be computed at once as matrix multiplications. GPUs handle this with massive parallelism.

O(1) path length

Between any two words, regardless of distance in the sentence

Self-attention is the key breakthrough because it solves ALL three RNN problems at once. First, no vanishing gradients: every word has a direct attention connection to every other word. The gradient only needs to pass through one attention layer, not through a chain of hidden states. Second, no bottleneck: instead of compressing everything into one vector, each word builds its representation by attending to all other words. Third, full parallelism: the attention scores for all word pairs are computed as a single matrix multiplication (Q times K-transpose). This is exactly what GPUs are optimized for. The path length between any two words is O(1), meaning a word at position 1 can directly attend to a word at position 1000. This is why Transformers handle long documents so well.

Part 4: Attention in Practice

Computation details and multi-head attention

Now let's go a bit deeper into how attention is used in practice. We will cover multi-head attention, computational complexity, and how attention weights can be visualized to understand model behavior.

Multi-Head Attention

"Attending to information from different representation subspaces"

Analogy: Multiple Reviewers
Imagine sending your code for review to 8 different experts. One checks for security issues, another for performance, another for readability. Each reviewer focuses on a different aspect. Multi-head attention is the same idea: multiple attention "heads" look at different patterns.

In practice, we do not use just one attention function. We use multiple "heads" in parallel. Each head has its own set of Q, K, V weight matrices. This means each head can learn to attend to different types of patterns. Research has shown that different heads actually do specialize: some learn syntactic dependencies, some learn coreference, some learn positional patterns. The outputs of all heads are concatenated and projected through a final weight matrix. The original Transformer uses 8 heads with d_k = 64 each (total dimension 512). This gives the model much more representational power than a single attention function.

Computational Complexity

Model Complexity per Layer Sequential Ops Max Path Length
RNN O(n · d2) O(n) O(n)
Self-Attention O(n2 · d) O(1) O(1)

Where n = sequence length, d = model dimension

Trade-off: Self-attention uses more computation per layer (O(n2)), but it is fully parallel (O(1) sequential steps). For typical NLP lengths, the parallelism wins massively.

The O(n2) cost is why long-context models (100K+ tokens) need special techniques like sparse attention, linear attention, or sliding window attention.

Let's compare the computational complexity. This table is from the original Transformer paper. RNNs have O(n) sequential operations, meaning you must wait n steps. Self-attention has O(1) sequential operations because everything is computed in parallel. However, self-attention has O(n-squared) total computation per layer because every word attends to every other word. For a sequence of 1000 words, that is 1 million attention scores. For typical NLP lengths (up to a few thousand tokens), the parallelism advantage dominates. But for very long sequences (100K+ tokens), the quadratic cost becomes a problem. This is why there is active research on efficient attention variants like sparse attention, linear attention, and sliding window attention used in models like Mistral.

Let's put everything in historical context. In 2014, Bahdanau showed that attention could dramatically improve machine translation by letting the decoder look back at all encoder states. In 2015, Luong simplified the scoring function with dot products. In 2017, Vaswani et al. published the landmark paper "Attention Is All You Need", which introduced the Transformer: an architecture built ENTIRELY on attention, with no RNNs at all. Self-attention replaced the sequential processing of RNNs. This was the paper that changed everything. From 2018 onwards, every major NLP model (BERT, GPT, T5, etc.) is based on the Transformer. Every large language model you use today, including the ones behind ChatGPT and Claude, is built on the attention mechanism we learned today.

Key Takeaways

1. RNNs have three fundamental limitations: vanishing gradients, information bottleneck, sequential processing

2. Attention lets models focus on relevant parts of the input instead of compressing everything into one vector

3. Bahdanau (additive) and Luong (multiplicative) attention improved seq2seq models, but the RNN backbone remained

4. Self-attention eliminated the need for RNNs entirely: every word attends to every other word in O(1) path length

5. The Query-Key-Value framework and scaled dot-product formula are the building blocks of all modern LLMs

Let's recap the five key takeaways from today. First, we understood why RNNs needed to be replaced: vanishing gradients, bottleneck, slow sequential processing. Second, attention is the elegant solution: look at all parts of the input, weighted by relevance. Third, Bahdanau and Luong attention were the first steps, applied to seq2seq translation. Fourth, self-attention was the breakthrough: it removes RNNs entirely and enables full parallelism. Fifth, the QKV framework and scaled dot-product formula are what power every LLM today. Here are the key references. The three foundational papers are Bahdanau 2014, Luong 2015, and Vaswani 2017 (the Transformer paper). For visual learners, Jay Alammar's Illustrated Transformer is the single best resource on the web. Lilian Weng's blog post is also excellent for a thorough mathematical treatment. I encourage you to skim at least the Illustrated Transformer before next session.
